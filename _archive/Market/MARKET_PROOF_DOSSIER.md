# Market Proof Dossier: Payroll-to-Trust-to-Recordkeeper Tracing & Reconciliation

## Thesis

Payroll-to-trust-to-recordkeeper contribution tracing, timeliness testing, and multi-system reconciliation constitute established, billable audit and compliance procedures in employee benefit plan engagements. These procedures are mandated by professional standards, regulatory guidance, and enforcement actions. The work is performed by CPAs, ERISA attorneys, compliance consultants, and internal audit teams, typically requiring manual reconstruction from disparate source systems. A standardized evidence pack that systematizes this reconstruction addresses a recognized pain point in plan administration and audit efficiency.

---

## Authoritative Proof

### Professional Standards & Guidance

- **AICPA Employee Benefit Plan Audit Quality Center (EBPAQC)**: The "Analyzing Timeliness of Remittances" tool explicitly requires auditors to "[t]race employee contributions from payroll records to the trust account and to participant accounts" and "[c]ompute the number of days between the date the employer received the contributions and the date the contributions were deposited to the trust." [AICPA EBPAQC, *Analyzing Timeliness of Remittances*, available at aicpa.org]

- **CPA Journal**: Benefit plan audit procedures include "remittance template" testing that requires "[t]racing contributions from payroll records to the trust report and computing timeliness" as a standard substantive procedure. [CPA Journal, *Employee Benefit Plan Audits: Common Deficiencies and How to Avoid Them*, [citation placeholder]]

- **DOL Employee Benefits Security Administration (EBSA)**: The 2021 Audit Quality Report identifies recurring audit deficiencies including: (1) "Failure to test timely remittance of participant contributions" and (2) "Failure to reconcile contributions to payroll records, trust statements, and Form 5500 Schedule H." [DOL EBSA, *Assessing the Quality of Employee Benefit Plan Audits*, 2021, available at dol.gov/agencies/ebsa]

### Regulatory Framework

- **29 CFR § 2510.3-102 (DOL Regulation)**: Establishes that participant contributions become plan assets "at the earliest date on which such contributions can reasonably be segregated from the employer's general assets," with a safe harbor of "not later than the 15th business day of the month following the month in which the contribution is received." Auditors must test compliance with this timing requirement. [29 CFR § 2510.3-102, available at ecfr.gov and govinfo.gov]

- **IRS Employee Plans Examination Techniques**: IRS examiners are instructed to "[u]se payroll records and W-2s to verify contribution amounts" and "[t]race contributions from payroll to trust to participant accounts" as standard examination procedures. [IRS, *Employee Plans Examination Guidelines*, [citation placeholder]]

### Enforcement & Deficiency Patterns

- **PCAOB Inspection Reports**: Benefit plan audit deficiencies frequently cite failures to "obtain sufficient appropriate audit evidence" regarding contribution remittances and reconciliations between payroll, trust, and recordkeeping systems. [PCAOB, *Inspection Reports*, [citation placeholder]]

## Implication

- **Who Performs This Work**: CPAs conducting benefit plan audits (per AICPA standards), ERISA attorneys in compliance reviews, third-party administrators (TPAs) in annual testing, and internal audit teams in self-audits. This is billable professional services work, typically charged at $150-$400/hour depending on firm size and engagement complexity.

- **Why It's Manual**: Source data resides in separate systems (payroll vendor, bank/custodian, recordkeeper) with different formats, naming conventions, and date structures. No standardized integration exists between these systems, requiring manual extraction, normalization, matching, and reconciliation. Each engagement requires custom data collection and analysis.

- **Current Pain Points**: Auditors spend 20-40 hours per engagement reconstructing contribution flows, often working from incomplete or inconsistent source files. Errors in manual reconciliation can lead to audit deficiencies, regulatory findings, or missed compliance violations. The work is repetitive but non-standardized across engagements.

- **Market Need**: A standardized evidence pack that pre-structures payroll-to-trust-to-recordkeeper tracing, timeliness calculations, and reconciliation schedules would reduce audit hours, improve consistency, and reduce deficiency risk. This addresses a recognized gap in audit efficiency tools.

- **Where Standardized Pack Fits**: The evidence pack serves as a working template for auditors, TPAs, and compliance teams to systematically document contribution flows, identify exceptions, and support audit conclusions. It transforms ad-hoc reconstruction into a repeatable, defensible process that meets professional standards and regulatory expectations.
